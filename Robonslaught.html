About:

Project Details

Robonslaught was developed as a prototype proof of concept using GameMaker Studio 2. The game centers on the concept of one player being confined to a small space while another player launches hazards from outside that space. This idea was inspired by the unique RPG battle system of the game Undertale.

While Robonslaught was envisioned primarily as a local multiplayer experience, it also includes single-player modes against AI opponents. The development process spanned approximately two months, beginning with simple sprites moving within a black void and ultimately evolving into the Project it is today, featuring a diverse array of weapons and custom sprites.

A little about the creation of this project

Game Loop:

As Robonslaught was developed as a proof of concept and diverged from traditional gaming conventions, it provided an excellent opportunity to explore a variety of innovative ideas, each presenting unique programming challenges. One significant challenge was implementing comprehensive controller support, as the game was designed for local multiplayer. I integrated full controller functionality throughout the project and tailored the main gameplay mechanics to utilize the joystick effectively.

Through the development of Robonslaught, I gained extensive experience in several key areas, including encapsulation, technical music control, collision detection, pixel-perfect collisions, input handling across multiple devices, and adherence to programming conventions.

Version 0.4:

In this iteration, I incorporated collision mechanics for the blue player, including interactions with projectiles and walls. This version also marked the introduction of health and timer mechanics that ultimately made it into the final release.

Additionally, I developed a dash mechanic for the blue player, which posed challenges for collision detection due to the rapid movement often leading to missed collision registrations. If given the opportunity to revisit Robonslaught, I would reintroduce the dash mechanic, leveraging my improved understanding of collision systems. Specifically, I would explore the implementation of a ray-tracking collision system to enhance accuracy.
